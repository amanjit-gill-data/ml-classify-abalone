Normality - Tabachnick and Lidell 4.1.5.1

Skewness and kurtosis: Skewness has to do with the symmetry of the distribution; a skewed variable is a variable whose mean is not in the center of the distribution. Kurtosis has to do with the peakedness of a distribution; a distribution is either too peaked (with short, thick tails) or too flat (with long, thin tails).

Because the standard errors for both skewness and kurtosis decrease with larger N, the null hypothesis is likely to be rejected with large samples when there are only minor deviations from normality. So if the sample is large, it is a good idea to look at the shape of the distribution instead of using formal inference tests.

Transformations - Tabachnick and Lidell 4.1.8

Moderately different from normal: try square root

Substantially different from normal: try logarithm

Severely different from normal: try inverse

For J-shape (i.e. severe), inverse delivers improved results even if normality isn’t achieved

If nothing improves the results, try dichotomising the variable instead.

Process - based on Tabachnick and Lidell 4.1.8

  1. Summarise univariate data
  • Identify out-of-range values ✅
  • Identify if values are missing; ✅

  1. Identify univariate outliers
  • histograms and boxplots ✅
  • z-scores

  1. Evaluate linearity and homoscedasticity
  • Bivariate scatterplots for both linearity and homoscedasticity ✅

(oval shape also implies univariate normality, which we’ll confirm in step 4) ✅

  1. Evaluate normality
  • Distribution plots with normal distribution as an overlay ✅
  • P-P plots
  • Mardia’s skewness and kurtosis ✅

  1. Perform repairs
  • Transformations
  • Re-check normality and repeat until achieved
  • Re-check outliers and remove if needed
  • See if removed outliers change summaries (measures of centre and spread); leave them in if they don’t change anything
